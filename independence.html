Path to Independence (1939-1947)

The final phase of India's struggle for freedom

World War II and the Freedom Struggle

The outbreak of World War II in 1939 created a complex political situation in India. The British government, without consulting Indian leaders, declared India to be at war with Germany. This unilateral decision intensified the demand for immediate independence and shaped the final phase of the freedom struggle.

The response to World War II revealed divergent strategies within the Indian national movement:

  • Congress Position: Initially refused to support the war effort without a clear commitment to Indian independence. Congress ministries in the provinces resigned in protest.
  • Muslim League Stance: Used the opportunity to build its position, cooperating with the British and consolidating support for a separate Muslim state.
  • Subhas Chandra Bose's Approach: Believed in taking advantage of Britain's weakened position during the war to gain independence. He escaped from house arrest and eventually formed the Indian National Army (INA) to fight alongside Japan against the British.
  • Communist Perspective: Initially opposed the war as "imperialist" but changed their position after Nazi Germany attacked the Soviet Union in 1941.

The Final Phase (1939-1947)

The period from 1939 to 1947 saw dramatic developments that accelerated the independence process:

1939-1940

Congress Provincial Ministries Resign

Following Britain's unilateral decision to involve India in World War II, all Congress ministries in the provinces resigned in protest. This political vacuum allowed the Muslim League to gain strength in many regions.

March 1940

Lahore Resolution

The Muslim League, under Muhammad Ali Jinnah, passed the Lahore Resolution (later known as the Pakistan Resolution), formally demanding separate Muslim-majority states in the northwestern and eastern zones of India.

This resolution laid the foundation for the eventual partition of India and the creation of Pakistan.

August 1942

Quit India Movement

In response to the failure of the Cripps Mission, Gandhi launched the Quit India Movement, calling for immediate British withdrawal from India. He gave the famous call: "Do or Die" â€“ either free India or die in the attempt.

The British responded with mass arrests of Congress leaders, leaving the movement without direction. Despite this, spontaneous protests erupted across the country, demonstrating the depth of anti-colonial sentiment.

1943-1945

Indian National Army and Subhas Chandra Bose

Subhas Chandra Bose escaped from house arrest, made his way to Germany and then to Japan, where he reorganized the Indian National Army (INA) composed of Indian prisoners of war. The INA fought alongside Japanese forces against the British in Burma, advancing as far as the northeastern borders of India.

"Give me blood, and I shall give you freedom!" - Subhas Chandra Bose

1945-1946

INA Trials

After Japan's defeat in World War II, captured INA officers were put on trial at the Red Fort in Delhi. The trials generated enormous public sympathy for the defendants and nationalist sentiment, even among the British Indian armed forces, causing serious concern for the British authorities.

February 1946

Royal Indian Navy Mutiny

Indian sailors of the Royal Indian Navy mutinied in Bombay and other coastal cities, raising nationalist flags on naval vessels. The mutiny, combined with other acts of resistance in the armed forces, convinced the British that they could no longer rely on Indian troops to maintain their rule.

March-June 1946

Cabinet Mission Plan

The British Cabinet Mission proposed a federal union of provinces with significant autonomy, attempting to keep India united. After initial agreement, the plan broke down due to differing interpretations by the Congress and the Muslim League.

August 1946

Direct Action Day and Communal Violence

The Muslim League called for Direct Action to achieve Pakistan, leading to severe communal riots, particularly in Calcutta (now Kolkata). The violence spread to other parts of the country, creating a climate of fear and mistrust between communities.

February 1947

Mountbatten Plan and Decision to Partition

Lord Mountbatten, the last Viceroy of India, announced Britain's intention to transfer power by June 1948. This timeline was later accelerated to August 1947. The decision to partition India was formally announced, leading to the creation of two independent nations: India and Pakistan.

Independence and Partition

On August 15, 1947, India finally achieved independence after nearly 200 years of British rule. However, the joy of freedom was tempered by the tragedy of partition, which resulted in one of the largest migrations in human history and widespread communal violence.

August 14-15, 1947

Transfer of Power

Pakistan came into existence on August 14, 1947, while India gained independence at midnight on August 15. In Delhi, Jawaharlal Nehru delivered his famous "Tryst with Destiny" speech to the Constituent Assembly:

"At the stroke of the midnight hour, when the world sleeps, India will awake to life and freedom. A moment comes, which comes but rarely in history, when we step out from the old to the new, when an age ends, and when the soul of a nation, long suppressed, finds utterance."

August-October 1947

Mass Migration and Communal Violence

The partition led to a mass migration of approximately 15 million people, with Hindus and Sikhs moving to India and Muslims to Pakistan. The migration was accompanied by horrific violence that claimed hundreds of thousands of lives. Gandhi worked tirelessly to restore peace, especially in Calcutta and Delhi.

January 30, 1948

Assassination of Mahatma Gandhi

Mahatma Gandhi was assassinated by Nathuram Godse, a Hindu extremist who opposed Gandhi's efforts to promote Hindu-Muslim harmony. His death was a profound shock to the newly independent nation and to the world.

Jawaharlal Nehru announced Gandhi's death to the nation with the words: "The light has gone out of our lives, and there is darkness everywhere."

The Challenges of Freedom

Independent India faced numerous challenges:

  • Refugee Rehabilitation: Resettling millions of refugees who had fled from what became Pakistan.
  • Integration of Princely States: Integrating over 560 princely states into the Indian Union, a task led by Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel.
  • Economic Development: Building an economy that could address widespread poverty and underdevelopment resulting from colonial exploitation.
  • Constitution-Making: Creating a constitution for the new nation that would reflect its democratic and secular aspirations.
  • Foreign Policy: Establishing India's position in international affairs during the emerging Cold War.

Despite these challenges, India succeeded in establishing a democratic system, maintaining unity amidst diversity, and setting the foundation for future development.

Legacy of the Freedom Struggle

India's struggle for independence left a lasting legacy:

  • It provided a model of non-violent resistance that inspired anti-colonial and civil rights movements worldwide.
  • It demonstrated the power of mass mobilization and the importance of inclusive leadership.
  • It shaped India's democratic institutions and political culture.
  • It established principles that continue to guide India's approach to international relations, including anti-imperialism, non-alignment, and peaceful coexistence.

"Long years ago we made a tryst with destiny, and now the time comes when we shall redeem our pledge... The achievement we celebrate today is but a step, an opening of opportunity, to the greater triumphs and achievements that await us." - Jawaharlal Nehru, August 15, 1947
